What to try in Morocco:

The average Moroccan gets 2/3 of his calories from cereals, mainly wheat, as well as barley. Couscous, bread, pastries are made from wheat, it is added to soups and porridges are made from it (in addition to wheat, there are also barley soups and porridges). Couscous is so important in Moroccan cuisine that in the central part of the country it is often called simply "food". Rice as a side dish on Moroccan tables is rare, puddings are made from it and stuffed with poultry and pies . Legumes are an important source of protein: lentils, chickpeas and garden beans: they are added to stews, and sandwich pastes are made from them. The most commonly used meat in Moroccan cuisine is mutton and chicken, as well as pigeon meat; fish is caught in coastal areas. Beef and veal are more popular than in other Arab countries. Milk is found mainly in the form of kefir, raeba, or soft cheese made from sheep's, cow's, or goat's milk; yogurt is unpopular.

Pastilla

Be sure to try this mouth-watering, fragrant, incredibly delicious traditional meat pie in Morocco. It is prepared on the basis of the thinnest puff pastry with a lot of tender juicy filling. In the classic recipe, the pie is stuffed with the meat of young pigeons (don't worry, not street pigeons – they are grown on special farms). It is perfectly complemented by onions, aromatic sauce, nuts, spices, sugar – it turns out an incredibly interesting combination of salty and sweet taste. This dish is easy to try in restaurants or eateries in Marrakech, Rabat, Fez. In hotels in Agadir, Essaouira, Tangier or other resorts in Morocco, the pie is most often prepared with chicken or fish. To cook this Moroccan dish in your kitchen, you should bring almonds, cinnamon, and a mixture of spices from Morocco.

Rgaif

Don't miss the national Moroccan breakfast of rgaif pancakes with various snacks. They are similar to Russian pancakes only in appearance – they are more like puff pastry pies with juicy filling. The filling can be anything: meat, cheese, sweet almond or honey (it turns out almost baklava), any other. And you can try pancakes without filling. The standard breakfast is complemented by bean paste, potato salad, spicy harissa pasta, vegetable salads and other snacks, as well as national mint tea. Recently, rgaif in Morocco has become an increasingly popular street food. Take a walk through the bazaars of Agadir, Marrakech or other Moroccan cities to find almonds, delicious honey and fragrant spices. In supermarkets you can buy harissa in glass jars. This will help you prepare a Moroccan breakfast at home.
